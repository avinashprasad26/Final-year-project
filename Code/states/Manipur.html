Famous arts and handicrafts from Manipur

Manipur is a north-eastern state of India. Imphal is the capital city. It is bounded to the north by Nagaland, to the south by Mizoram, and to the west by Assam. It also shares borders with two Myanmar regions: Sagaing Region to the east and Chin State to the south. The state has a total area of 22,327 square kilometres (8,621 sq mi).


Manipur's economy is primarily agrarian, with significant hydroelectric power generation potential. Manipur is the birthplace of Manipuri dance and is credited with introducing polo to Europeans. As of 2019, the state of Manipur is divided into 16 districts.

Art and Crafts

1: Cane and bamboo crafts

1. Manipur's cane and bamboo crafts are popular due to the abundance of bamboo. Furthermore, due to the high demand in the international market, production is high. Bamboo and cane are used to make lightweight items such as mats, baskets, lightweight furniture, stools, ashtrays, flower vases, and so on. You can decorate your home with these products to give it an ethnic look. These items are both inexpensive and attractive. Bamboo and cane furniture can also be used in homes and offices.

2: Kauna crafts

2. The kauna craft of Manipur is well known throughout the country. People from all over the country come to Manipur to buy kauna crafts because it is the golden land of kauna craft. Kauna craft is one of the state's most popular handicrafts, and kauna products are frequently given as gifts to friends and family. Kauna is a very delicate type of wood. Wood can be found in woodlands and forests. Kauna products are reasonably priced and considered accessible to the majority of people. Kauna is also used in the production of a variety of other handicrafts. Kauna products are gaining popularity by the day. As a result, Sangai Bazaar is attempting to bring the best Kauna products to their customers across India. Kauna Products sells a wide range of products such as watches, clothing, bags, wallets, and so on. Kauna Products is an Indian brand available in Sangai Bazaar.

3: Kauna reed mats

3. The double-weave mat known as the kaunaphak reed mat is a unique Manipur craft. The reed used to weave the mat is known as kauna in Meithei, a Manipuri tribal language. Locals make the mat for their own use as well as for commercial purposes. Phak is a yellow-colored succulent stem of a plant that grows in water. Reeds for the mats are carefully selected. Only when the plant has reached maturity is it cut. After being cut, the stems dry and become soft, pithy, and brittle. A bunch of cut stems of the appropriate length are then woven with bamboo placed at appropriate distances to create the desired length of the mat. The border is about an inch wide and has an interesting pattern. The kauna reed mats should not be washed because the reed deteriorates when exposed to moisture.

4: Black/Longpi pottery

4. Longpi pottery, also known as stone pottery or black pottery, is a traditional Manipur craft. The original name, Longpi pottery, is derived from the village of Longpi in Manipur. This is where the Tangkhul Naga tribe creates their beautiful earthenware. The people of Manipur's Ukhrul district attribute the origins of this craft to Goddess Panthoibi, the mother of artifact-making. She represents a stage in our evolution. Longpi Pottery is therefore required for performing rituals on festive occasions such as childbirth and marriage. Manipuri pottery is distinct in both style and technique. The material used is a three-to-one mixture of clay and powdered stone â€“ Black Serpentite stone and weathered rock. Serpentite rock provides strength, and weathered rock acts as a binding agent. The resulting paste is then hand-rolled into desired shapes. The potter's wheel is not used by the Longpi. All shaping is done by hand and with the assistance of moulds. The potter then moves the clay around, shaping and forming the pot. A rounded stone is used to support the pot from the inside, and it is beaten to the desired shape and thickness.

5: Block printing

5. The art of printing different patterns on fabrics using patterned blocks is known as block printing. This art form employs natural colours and is used to create bedsheets, sarees, and other textiles. This handicraft demonstrates the incredible talent of Manipur's artisans, who can make any fabric look extraordinary. This art form draws both locals and tourists. Traditionally, village chiefs were given a 'Khamen Chatpa,' which is a handprinted block towel. Manipur's traditional ceremonies feature block-printed dresses.
