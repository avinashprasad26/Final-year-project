Famous arts and handicrafts from Uttar Pradesh

Uttar Pradesh is a state in northern India. Lucknow is the capital city, Depending on their region of origin, residents of the state are known as Awadhi, Bagheli, Bhojpuri, Braji, Bundeli, Kannauji, or Rohilkhandi. Along with Urdu, Hindi is the most widely spoken language and the state's official language. Most of the major political entities that existed in ancient and mediaeval India were based in Uttar Pradesh, including the Maurya Empire, Harsha Empire, Gupta Empire, Pala Empire, Delhi Sultanate, Mughal Empire, and many others. Several holy Hindu temples and pilgrimage sites can be found in the state. Uttar Pradesh has three World Heritage sites and is India's top tourist destination. Agra, Allahabad, Aligarh, Ayodhya, Kushinagar, Varanasi, and Vrindavan are among the historical, natural, and religious tourist destinations in Uttar Pradesh.


The terrain of Uttar Pradesh region consists mostly is part of Northern plains covering an area of 243,286 km². As of 2019, the state of Uttar Pradesh is divided into 75 districts.

India has been blessed with artists who create finest of handicrafts that are renowned across the world. Uttar Pradesh is no different with artists and artisans specialised in different forms of art from Agra to Varanasi. Mughals ruled major parts of UP for over two centuries which impacted the artwork. The Nawabs were believed to have great taste and appreciation of art which led to further development of the craftsmanship in the region. Even today UP is home to several different handicrafts which are exported across the nation and the world. 1. Chikankari Embroidery

An exquisite specimen of Uttar Pradesh handicrafts, Chikan work has a long history that goes back to the early third century during the reign of Chandragupta Maurya. Chikan literally means embroidery, but the Chikan work involves 36 different types of stitches. It is a multistep process, with the first step being cutting of the cloth as per the desired shape. It is followed by block printing the core design on which the artist would complete the embroidery. Later, it is washed to remove the ink of the printed pattern. Some of the most common stitches include the Jali, Tepchi, Murri, Pashni, and Khatao. Chikan Kurti and Lucknow Chikan sarees are particularly popular.

2. Varanasi Brocade sarees

Varanasi Brocade or Varanasi Silk is another famous handicraft of Uttar Pradesh. Varanasi brocade saree is made of fine silk, cotton fabric with gold and silver threads making a different types of designs and patterns. The silver background with gold threads form geometrical designs called ‘butidar’ and ‘jaal’ adds beauty to these Varanasi saris. The Banaras saris play a very important role in any Indian marriage, as brides are supposed to wear them in their wedding ceremonies. The style patterns of these Banarasi saris and suits include elegant floral and leaf patterns, kalga, and bel. The designs of the motifs vary according to the durability of the material. The heavy work on the fabric shows a royal appearance.

3. Stonecraft

The Stone Craft of Uttar Pradesh deserves a mention when it comes to talking about the wonderful arts and handicrafts of Uttar Pradesh. This stonecraft skill flourished abundantly, especially under the rule of the Mughals. The Taj Mahal in Agra city of Uttar Pradesh is the most beautiful example of stone craftsmanship. The white-marble mausoleum is a magnificent sight, one of the wonders of the world, and a UNESCO World Heritage Site. This is an art in which craftsmen carved stone to give different shapes for windows, garden furniture, decorative arts, and so on. The must-visit places to buy and see this stone handicraft of Uttar Pradesh are Agra, Varanasi, and Fatehpur Sikri.

4. Carpet Weaving

Bhadoi, Shahjahanpur, and Mirzapur are the hubs for the production of carpets in Uttar Pradesh. Carpet weaving is a common task for most people in these regions and it has become one of the greatest art and handicraft of Uttar Pradesh. This practice that prevailed during Persian time has remained alive until now and has earned national and international fame. You might be able to see the finest silk carpets crafted with delicate Persian motifs across the state. The carpets are designed in a number of remarkable styles of flora, fauna, and other patterns.

Carpets of Uttar Pradesh are significantly better-known all across the world for its distinctive colors and styles. Employment in Uttar Pradesh constitutes nearly ninety percent of the carpet weavers. Bhadohi, Mirzapur and metropolis are the foremost carpet hubs in Uttar Pradesh. Among these places, Bhadohi enjoys price special mention because the economy of this district and its five hundred odd villages are utterly dependent upon the carpet business. The carpets of Uttar Pradesh associate with hand imprinted or hand engraved borders in rose-beige, honey, ivory and Republic of Iraq green. Another common design of the carpets created in Uttar Pradesh is named 'culpture design' that uses clippings of the thread round the helm and offers it a three-dimensional texture. largely bright coloured pastel shades are utilized in this eighteenth century style pattern. metropolis in Uttar Pradesh was the primary carpet center within the Mughal India. Twisted cotton thread is employed at the side of the Jute twine therefore giving rougher qualities of carpet with concerning sixty knots per sq. inch. Cotton and woolen carpets are primarily factory-made in Shahjehanpur and Agra wherever weavers manufacture long-established and conjointly new motifs, keeping the market demand in mind.

5. Brassware

Uttar Pradesh is India’s main production state for brass and copper articles. Etawah, Banaras, Sitapur are famous for these types of metal artifacts. Moradabad is also very popular in U. P for its metalwork, colors, and intricate engravings.

There are two types of metal engravings, one on the tinned surface is called Nakshi, while the other is known as khudai and is done on unpolished brass coated surface with lac. The pointed steel pencil is used to do khudai style of pattern. The metal brassware consists of traditional vases, Ganeshas, laughing Buddhas, stools, trays, and beautiful contemporary bowls. The Nataraja is the most beautiful item to be gifted and maintained as a suitable item in the brassware handicraft of Uttar Pradesh.

6. Pottery

Khurja is a traditional Indian ceramic art that takes place in the Bulandshahr area of Uttar Pradesh. 'The Ceramic Town' is another name for it. It is well-known for its material clay-based pottery. It is a handcrafted and hand-painted handicraft item. Khurja pottery dates back 500 years to the reign of the Mughal emperor. The following are the steps involved in making Khurja pottery: 1) clay preparation 2) shaping clay 3) smoothing and painting 4) The glazing procedure 5) The burning process. Khurja pots have a number of advantages. Its clay pots are wonderful for cooking food because they maintain the PH balance, making the meal less acidic and retaining all of the nutrients. Multani Kumhars are the name given to them by potters due of their origin in Multan. This innovative pottery provides a living for a large number of Khurja artisans. Currently, the city has almost 500 manufacturers producing Khurja artwork, such as pots, vases, dinnerware, tiles, and other ceramic products. The Central Glass and Ceramic Research Institute is the most well-known ceramic work institute in Khurja nowadays. Khurja pottery has established itself not only in the Indian market, but also in the worldwide market.

7. Sanjhi Art

Sanjhi art is a type of stencilling that originated in Mathura, Krishna's hometown. It is regarded as one of the finest forms of spiritual expression because of its inherent spiritual meanings that extend beyond immediate visual appeal. Sanjhi themes were used to embellish the walls and floors of temples in the 16th and 17th centuries. Sanjhi is derived from the Hindi word sandhya, which refers to the period of dusk when the art form is most commonly associated. The art depicts several Indian mythological stories, with a particular focus on Krishna's Leela. According to legend, Radhe created Sanjhi rangolis out of natural hues, flowers, leaves, and coloured stones to woo Krishna. Soon after, the other gopis followed suit, creating complex decorations to dazzle Krishna. Sanjhi designs are created by cutting stencils out of paper (usually handmade) with specially crafted scissors. These stencils are used to design rangoli patterns on flat surfaces or in water. After that, dry colours are dusted onto the surface. As important as cutting the design is filling the colours and lifting the stencils. Common motifs include peacocks, bullock carts, horses, cows, butterflies, and trees. The artist's passion and profound love for the Blue God is reflected in the meticulous craftsmanship. An complex Sanjhi artwork could take anything from an hour to a month to complete.
